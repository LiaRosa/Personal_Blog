A short narrative on my childhood. Written originally as a college entrance essay and later edited and changed into a more poetical format for an English class at said college.

Vocabulary:

Curandero - Healer

Abuelita - Grandmother

Abuelito - Grandfather

Ñaña - Sister 

Ruda - Rue

La Curandera: a girl playing healer


Curandero is a term used throughout Ecuador to describe a person who is skilled in the art of herbal medicine and “magical” plants. They are often deemed as healers in my community, using plants to cleanse the body of any evil; of sickness. Growing up in such a community, I was no stranger to these practices, practices that were performed in my household. I remember being a young child, wide-eyed, staring as my abuelita would pass an egg over my ñaña’s frail body. La pasada del huevo is supposed to get rid of any malice, especially that of the evil eye, that children are exposed to easily. After you roll the egg, prayers falling from your lips, intent to heal, present, you crack the egg into a cup of water. Based on the forming of the yolk and whites, you can determine the cause of ailment. 


I take an egg, my hands cupped around it, 

My hands cradling it, as if life were to hatch.

I enter the dark room, my eyes fixed on a sleeping form.

Whose form? Who lies on this white bed— a bed with wheels, a bed on wheels. 

The dripping of a bag going through a line; drip — drip — drip—

Hushed prayers being uttered from my lips, lips that should be producing laughter, 

Lips, child-lips, lips that have cried and that now murmur.

I pass the egg over this clothed body—

My abuelito lays here, he sleeps here, in this strange house,

With strange people, and now a strange girl hovers next to him, holding an egg. 


 Besides la pasada del huevo there are many techniques that are used, requiring medicinal plants. Plants like ruda, hierbabuena, eucalipto, and tabaco, are brushed against the body, purifying the soul. Infusing them in teas and baths, ensures your protection. Hanging them in dried batches can grant you wealth and prosperity. You can not be born in Ecuador without becoming one with her animals, with her hills, with her bountiful nature. I used to consider myself a friend of the plants, proudly telling my American friends that I knew secrets: I knew what the birds would sing, what the trees would say in hushed winds, I could feel the rain before the first drop; I was a child of earth; as a friend of nature, I pestered my grandpa with endless questions about herbs, and all the little critters that hid under rocks and the creatures that stalked from the trees. I wanted to know why the world came to be, and why plants provided us medicine. 


    Who are you? 

 Where am I? — 

Was I wrong? My shaky hands clenched behind my red dress,

Red like the color of his car; red like his favorite shirt;

Red like crimson, the crimson that bloomed on the ground.

Eyes downcast, lips pursed.

Why didn’t it work? It should have worked?

Warm the egg. Check. 

Have intent. Check.

Pray. Check.

Why – why – why did nothing change?


I quickly realized that I wouldn’t be able to become a curandera, so I had shifted my interest from medicinal herbs and focused on flowers and the language surrounding them. Much like how a word is a symbol with a prescribed definition, flowers all have different meanings and interpretations that can be used to communicate and form a message with. The idea of communicating with nature, through nature, became my secret code, one that I began to share with my abuelito. I would laugh before my abuelito would tell me to hush, both of us straining our ears to the trees, feet grounded, spine straight; one must stand like a tree to become one. 


    Trees can be uprooted,

Their roots exposed, what once was mighty

Now the fallen —

But, is it me who is the fallen?

If a tree falls and no one is around to hear.

Did it fall? Did I fall?

When you fell like an uprooted tree, 

Did you make a sound? Your red leaves veiling you


A brain can bleed, a violent crimson can pour from your skull, who knew a skull could be fragile, who knew— A hundred combinations of plants and yet you pain. How many eggs have been used and yet, you do not remember?  How a person whom you have raised can become a stranger, a strange little girl who cradles an egg. You can’t help but look at this sad girl, wondering why she cries, wondering why she holds onto this egg as if it were a lifeline. Little strange girl, don’t cry.
